A Comprehensive Reform Strategy to Reduce Police Use of Force

Brinda Sreenivas

Police use of force, particularly excessive force, has emerged as a systemic problem within American law enforcement, casting a long shadow over community trust, public safety and the integrity of the criminal justice system. The use of force by law enforcement officers is an inherent aspect of their duty. Yet, it is contentious. Recent statistics indicate that a substantial number of use of force incidents result in serious injuries or fatalities that disproportionately affect marginalized communities. The public's perception of police violence has led to increased scrutiny and demands for accountability, highlighting the need for a comprehensive reform, and the urgency stems from the profound social, economic, and legal consequences of unchecked force, which undermines the principles of justice and equality. By understanding the nature of this problem, its scope, root causes, and the necessity for reform, we can develop effective strategies to mitigate these incidents and foster a more just and equitable law enforcement system.

The nature of police use of force as a systemic issue lies in its pervasive and institutionalized character. Excessive force, defined as the unwarranted or disproportionate application of physical, verbal, or psychological coercion, is not merely the act of individual “bad apples” but a reflection of broader systemic failures. This problem is intertwined with racial disparities, for example; the 1991 beating of Rodney King, the 1999 shooting of Amadou Diallo and the 2014 killing of Michael Brown reveal patterns of racial profiling and aggressive policing tactics (Lyle & Esmail, 2016). Incidents captured on video expose a culture where force is too readily employed, particularly against African Americans, who, despite comprising 13% of the U.S. population, accounted for 31% of police shooting victims in 2015. The systemic nature is further evidenced by the “code of silence” within police departments, where 25% of officers believe whistleblowing is futile, and 50% report colleagues ignoring misconduct (Lyle & Esmail, 2016). This protects rogue officers and perpetuates a cycle of impunity, making excessive force a recurring feature of policing rather than an anomaly.

The scope of this problem’s impact is staggering, affecting communities, economies, and the legitimacy of law enforcement itself. Consequences of excessive force extend far beyond individual victims, it erodes trust and legitimacy particularly within communities of color contributing to a cycle of trauma and instability. Studies indicate that police violence can lead to a decline in resident calls to 911 in affected neighborhoods and jeopardize public safety. Economically, police brutality costs America millions of dollars in legal settlements and judgments, diverting public funds from community development and exacerbating urban inequality. Lumsden’s analysis underscores the immense financial burden, with cities paying billions in settlements; $5.7 billion from 2004 to 2014 across major U.S. cities, including $1 billion in New York City alone (Lumsden, 2017). Police use of force also raises serious legal and constitutional issues that fall within the scope of criminal law as they often violate the Fourth Amendment. Officers can face criminal charges like assault or even murder when their use of force is deemed unlawful. This puts a significant amount of strain on the legal system because of the difficulty in securing convictions due to legal protections like qualified immunity.

Understanding the root causes is crucial for effective reform. Police subculture often fosters an "us vs. them" mentality, where officers may view certain communities as adversaries (Lyle & Esmail, 2016). A culture that prioritizes aggressive tactics over community engagement can normalize the use of force. Many escalated encounters involve individuals experiencing mental health crises or substance abuse. Structurally, inadequate training and militarized policing exacerbate the problem. Officers frequently receive minimal de-escalation training compared to extensive firearms instruction, prioritizing force over conflict resolution (Palmiotto, 2017). Moreover, a lack of clear use-of-force policies and broad immunity protections often shield officers from accountability, creating an environment where excessive force is normalized without consequences.

Proposing a comprehensive reform strategy to tackle cultural, structural, and legal barriers, such as the “code of silence” and qualified immunity, and reduce use-of-force incidents by enhancing existing tools like Body-Worn Cameras (BWCs) and Implicit Bias Training (IBT), and integrating them in a new, interlocking systemic framework powered by technology. This strategy aims to create a self-correcting ecosystem for policing built on three core functions;

  1. Predictive co-responder systems created by integrating pervasive AI technology into 911/dispatch systems to analyze call data and dispatch most appropriate responders effectively reducing officer involvement in 60-70% of mental health calls, effectively preventing escalation pathways.
  2. BWCs equipped with real-time accountability modules to help police officers check their stance, tone, de-escalation compliance, and learn from post-incident problem detection eliminating recall bias.
  3. Replace static re-certification with a continuous accountability loop built on a dynamic, data-driven performance measurement system. Recertification would require police officers to meet specific metrics, such as fewer force incidents, higher BWC de-escalation compliance scores, and zero sustained bias complaints.

BWCs are implemented as a direct response to public demand for greater transparency and accountability. BWCs provide recorded video that serves as crucial evidence in resolving disputes and investigating allegations of misconduct or excessive use of force (Machado & Lugo, 2022). BWCs may work like a deterrent and lead to a reduction in use of force incidents, as the knowledge of being recorded can induce a "civilizing effect" on both parties (Machado & Lugo, 2022). BWCs also provide valuable content for officer training and performance review, offering real-world scenarios for instructional purposes and helping supervisors identify areas for improvement in de-escalation techniques and overall conduct. However, their implementation, while offering clear advantages, also presents a nuanced set of challenges, particularly when viewed through the lens of a "moral panic" framework. "The public debate about body cameras must reflect the nuance and complexity of camera policy, grounded in the limitations of video evidence and the hard questions of implementation" (Wasserman, 2015). Significant privacy concerns arise for both officers and the public and the vast amounts of data generated by BWCs presents substantial financial and logistical challenges for police departments. There is also the potential for BWC footage to be selectively used, which can manipulate public perception rather than ensure genuine accountability. The absence, or ambiguity, of video in critical encounters can itself become a new source of controversy. The lack of standardized national policies further contributes to disparities in how BWCs are utilized and their ultimate impact across different jurisdictions.

Implicit Bias Training (IBT) is adopted by numerous law enforcement agencies as a strategy to address and mitigate the impact of unconscious biases on policing outcomes. IBT can raise awareness among officers about the existence and influence of implicit biases (Machado & Lugo, 2022). By understanding that biases can operate outside conscious awareness, officers may become more vigilant in their interactions and decision-making. Furthermore, IBT can foster a culture of introspection and self-awareness within police departments, encouraging officers to critically examine their own perceptions and reactions. Despite the theoretical promise, the effectiveness of IBT in consistently reducing racial bias in police use of force is a subject of mixed evidence and considerable debate (Machado & Lugo, 2022). A significant disadvantage is that simply raising awareness of bias does not always translate into sustained behavioral change, particularly in high-stress, rapidly evolving situations. Critics argue that IBT might be perceived as a "check-the-box" solution rather than a fundamental shift in institutional practices. A key gap in IBT implementation is the lack of standardized curriculum and rigorous evaluation. Without consistent, long-term measurement of behavioral outcomes, it is challenging to determine if IBT leads to lasting reductions in biased actions or merely a temporary change in attitudes. The complexity of human bias means that IBT, in isolation, may not be a sufficient solution to reform deeply entrenched systemic issues within law enforcement.

Compared to BWCs, the real-time accountability module integrates BWCs but elevates them from passive recording devices to proactive compliance and prediction tools. While traditional BWCs document events and offer transparency, These modules leverage AI to analyze footage in real-time for de-escalation prompts and post-incident for automated bias detection, providing objective evidence that a standalone BWC does not. Furthermore, predictive co-responder systems prevent situations where BWCs might even be needed by diverting calls from traditional police response altogether. Continuous accountability loop goes beyond mere awareness-based IBT. While IBT aims to alter perceptions (Machado & Lugo, 2022), continuous accountability loop enforces consequences through data-driven metrics for recertification, directly tying compliance to outcomes. This approach addresses the critique that training alone may not overcome entrenched cultural barriers or guarantee application in high-stress environments, and moves from general awareness to a system of continuous, measurable accountability for bias-related behaviors. While the proposed reform framework offers a sophisticated approach to force reduction through structural and technological intervention, its success hinges on overcoming significant financial, ethical, and organizational hurdles.

In conclusion, police use of force is a systemic crisis rooted in historical racism, cultural dysfunction, inadequate training, and legal protections. The moral, social, economic, and legal imperatives, supported by scholarly and authoritative sources, underscore the need for comprehensive changes, from cultural shifts to policy overhauls. Without action, the cycle of violence and alienation will persist, undermining the promise of equitable policing. Reform is not just necessary, it is essential to uphold democracy and protect communities.
